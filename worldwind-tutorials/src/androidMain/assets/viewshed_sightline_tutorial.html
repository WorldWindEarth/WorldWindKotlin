Viewshed Sightline Tutorial

Demonstrates how to create a ViewshedSightline — a per-cell visibility analysis computed against the source elevation model rather than the rendered terrain mesh. Sampling resolution is decoupled from the camera's terrain LoD, so far-away coverage areas remain accurate even when the visible terrain tiles are coarse.

Two viewshed footprints are placed around Mount St. Helens to show contrasting line-of-sight scenarios:

  • Circular viewshed — observer hovers ~120 m above the south crater rim at the geometric center of the crater, with a 15 km radius. Visible terrain (green) covers the crater floor and the ridges within line of sight; occluded terrain (gray) is in the radio shadow of the rim.
  • Rectangular viewshed — observer sits 30 m above Swift Reservoir, with a 14 km × 4 km E-W rectangle aligned to the long axis of the reservoir. Visibility forms a bright corridor along the water (blue) while the canyon walls cast clean shadows.

Each placemark is draggable. Move it to a peak and the visible region grows to the full AOI — that's correct line-of-sight from a summit observer, not a kernel artifact.

The kernel uses an Amanatides-Woo grid traversal per cell with bilinear elevation sampling and an earth-curvature + atmospheric-refraction correction, so visibility verdicts on long sightlines remain accurate.
